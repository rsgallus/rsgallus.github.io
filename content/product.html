Product in a nutshell

Last updated July 8th, 2021

Your highest​-order contribution and responsibility as a product manager is to make sure what the engineers are asked to build will be worth building.

Some opening thoughts

Product Management is one of those complicated roles where everyone does it a little differently. There are many ways to do it right and even more ways to do it wrong. That makes it challenging to learn and to teach. Marty Cagan's quote at the top is, in my opinion, the best summary of Product Management - making sure the software is worth building. Let's talk about how to do that, starting with some basics.

Some basic ideas

Agile is a set of principles for building software that focuses on delivering working pieces quickly so we can learn from it. This idea was first published in 2001 by a group of software engineers, in a document called the Agile Manifesto​, which is definitely worth a quick read. Since then, others have expanded on the idea.

Scrum is a popular framework to help us achieve Agile software development. In the Scrum model, we plan our software development work into tiny cycles called Sprints, which are usually two weeks long. At the end of each Sprint, we do a Retrospective to reflect on what went well in the process and what could be improved. The important thing to know about Scrum is that we can break all the rules as long as it helps us be more Agile.

What makes software worth building?

Marty Cagan explains this best in his book Inspired when he says software needs to be valuable, usable, feasible, viable for our business.

How do we test for value?

There are things we can do to increase our confidence that a product or feature will be valuable, but the truth is we can never know for sure until we put something in front of real users.

How do I prioritize?

Prioritize specific business outcomes, not features or generic business value

Product Managers frequently talk about prioritization being one of the harder parts of the job. We try to guess at "business value" and priotize a list of features by that. Here's the secret though - you shouldn't prioritize by features at all. Work with stakeholders and company leadership to prioritize business outcomes. Then work on the features you are most confident will impact that business outcome.
